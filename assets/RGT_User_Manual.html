Realized Genetic Gain Trials — Dashboard User Manual

Everything you need to read, interrogate and report on the IFC Douglas-fir Realized Gain Trials: what the tool is, the science behind it, every plot and table, the report assistant, the equations, and how to run it. Written for the workshop.

1What it is, why it exists, and who it's for

The RGT dashboard turns ~86,400 tree measurements into clear answers to one practical question: is genetically improved Douglas-fir seed worth deploying, where, and how confident can we be?

What

An interactive web dashboard (Plotly Dash) that compares genetically Improved seedlots against local Woods Run (unimproved) checks across Douglas-fir installations in two regions, over three measurement years and three growth metrics — and quantifies the realized genetic gain, its statistical significance, and the survival trade-off.

Why

Progeny tests predict gain; only a Realized Gain Trial confirms it under operational conditions. Members need a defensible, at-a-glance read on whether to plant improved stock — and a way to generate a written, numbers-grounded report for a stand, region or seedlot in a couple of minutes.

Who should use it

AudienceWhat they get
Co-op members / forestersA deploy / hold call per site, mortality risk, and a downloadable report.
Researchers / biometriciansEffect sizes, confidence intervals, significance (raw & FDR-adjusted), G×E.
Managers / fundersRegional summaries and trend reports without touching the raw data.

Where it lives

It runs in a browser. Hosted at ifc.nkn.uidaho.edu/dashapp/ for members, and can be run locally by the IT/host team (see §12). No install is needed for everyday users — just a link.

2The trial behind the numbers

The dashboard is a window onto a field experiment. Understanding the design makes every chart read clearly.

The comparison

At each installation, two seed sources are planted side by side:

  • Improved — first-generation elite families selected from IETIC progeny trials (top volume performers).
  • Woods Run — local, unimproved seed native to the same seed zone — the operational baseline (the "check").

Their difference in growth, expressed as a percentage of the Woods Run baseline, is the realized genetic gain.

The layout of one installation

Each installation has 6 plots. Plots 1–3 are Woods Run; plots 4–6 are Improved. Each plot is an inner 10×10 monitored grid (100 trees) inside a 12×12 planting, the outer ring acting as a buffer. Ten elite families and ten woods-run sources are randomly planted and tracked individually, so the dashboard can compare both bulk and individual seedlot performance.

Why this matters for the stats: because source is confounded with physical plot, the plots are pseudo-replicates. The genuine independent unit is the seedlot (genetic entry) mean — which is exactly what the significance tests use (see §9).

The mean cards show the sample size (seedlots and trees) beneath them — that context is what makes a significance star honest. "Realized gain" is coloured green when positive, red when negative.

5.2 The deployment decision card

Directly below the KPIs, a colour-coded verdict for this installation across all years and metrics:

Deploy  significant positive gain, acceptable survival  ·  Deploy with caution  gains positive but unproven, or mortality high  ·  Hold  significant negative gain, or mostly non-positive

It is deterministic, not the AI — computed from the latest measured year of each metric plus the mortality gap. It lists the evidence it used (per-metric gain, significance, mortality) so nothing is a black box, and ends with a reminder to confirm with the full report before an operational call.

5.3 The six per-plot growth maps

Six heatmaps reconstruct the field layout of each plot — Woods Run plots 1–3, Improved plots 4–6 — placing every tree by its planting position on the 10-wide monitored grid. Each cell is coloured by growth and labelled with its seedlot; hover for replication, defect and management codes. All six share one colour scale so Woods and Improved are directly comparable. (A layout reconstruction from planting order, not surveyed coordinates.)

5.4 Tables & the avg / max / min chart

  • Mean by plot (Woods, Improved) — each seedlot's mean in each plot.
  • Avg / Max / Min by seedlot — a marker at each seedlot's mean with whiskers to its plot range (this is a range, not a confidence interval).
Growth means here exclude dead and replacement trees — see §9.2. The heatmaps still show every planted position (including dead) because they are a spatial map.

6Genetic Gain & Summary — across all sites

Answers: "Across the sites in this region / year / metric, how big is the gain, where, how certain, and at what survival cost?" The tab is organised into four labelled sections.

6.1 · Realized genetic gain

A KPI row (number of installations, mean & median gain, count of significant sites, and the gain-vs-productivity relationship), then two charts:

  • Woods Run → Improved dumbbell — one row per site. A Woods dot and an Improved dot joined by a connector: green when gain is positive, red when negative. A gold ring on the Improved dot marks statistical significance (p<0.05). A grey whisker is the 95% confidence interval of the gain — when it reaches back across the Woods dot, the gain is not significant.
  • Gain vs site productivity — does gain depend on site quality? The x-axis is the Woods Run site mean (a productivity proxy); the y-axis is absolute gain (Improved − Woods, in metric units). Gold points are significant; a dashed line is the fitted regression with its r and p.
The FDR toggle. Each site is tested separately, so across many sites a few can look significant by chance. Tick FDR-adjust to apply Benjamini–Hochberg across the visible sites; stars, gold rings and the "p" column then reflect multiplicity-controlled q-values. Exports follow the toggle (filename gets a _FDR suffix).

6.2 · Site-by-site detail

  • Site gain & survival table — per site: Woods & Improved means, Gain % (coloured by sign), stars, p (or q), and both mortality columns. Sortable and filterable.
  • Per-seedlot means & mortality — every seedlot in the view, with mean, standard error and mortality %.
  • Download buttons export either table as CSV for the current filters.

6.3 · Distributions across seedlots & sites

  • Seedlot means by site (Woods, Improved) — grouped bars; each site keeps its colour across the two charts.
  • Distribution of tree growth by seedlot — boxplots (box = IQR, whiskers 1.5×IQR, points = outliers), split by source.
  • Installation means ± SE — horizontal bars ranking sites, Woods vs Improved with standard-error bars.

6.4 · Survival & damage

The trial's #1 member priority — establishment survival — lives here:

  • Mortality by site — share of trees coded dead/replaced, Woods vs Improved, per installation (lower is better).
  • Top damage agents by source — the most common defect codes (browse, frost, herbicide, unhealthy…) as a % of each source's trees.
Read gain and survival together. Improved stock often grows faster and dies a little more; the deployment call weighs both.

8ForestTask — the report assistant

A chat assistant (bottom-right) with two modes: quick answers, and full written reports — both grounded strictly in the numbers the dashboard computes (it never invents figures).

💬 Quick questions

Type a short question for an instant inline answer.

  • "What's the gain at HOODOO in Year 2?"
  • "Mortality at SITKA?"
  • "Plot the gains" → a quick chart inline.

Short look-ups use a fast model; analytical questions escalate automatically.

📄 Reports

Start with the word report (or pick a suggestion chip). A panel opens on the right, plays a short build animation, and produces a full document — charts, tables and written analysis — in ~1–2 minutes, then offers an HTML download.

  • "report on installation HOODOO"
  • "deployment report for CORE sites"
  • "report comparing INW vs K-S"
  • "report on seedlot 97-72 across INW"

What a report contains

The engine first computes the evidence deterministically (figures & tables, including year-by-year trend charts), then an LLM writes the narrative around those fixed numbers, a second pass critiques it against an expert rubric, and a third revises it. Sections: Executive summary · Realized genetic gain · Site/installation detail · Seedlot detail · Plot-level & spatial pattern · Survival & defects · Key findings · Caveats & recommendations — followed by the computed Evidence, Deployment decision, Seedlot stability (G×E) and Damage & risk sections.

It understands scope. Name any combination of region / installation / seedlot / year / metric, ask for a comparison or a deployment decision, and it confines the report to exactly that. Mistyped a seedlot id? It finds the closest real one and says so.
Offline? If no model key is configured, ForestTask still produces a report from the computed numbers using built-in templates — just without the polished prose.

9Methods & equations

Every number in the dashboard comes from the formulas below. They're also summarised in-app under the ⓘ Methods button.

9.1 Realized genetic gain

For an installation × year × metric, with \(\mu_I\) the mean of Improved seedlot means and \(\mu_W\) the mean of Woods Run seedlot means:

$$\text{Gain}\,\%=100\times\frac{\mu_I-\mu_W}{\mu_W}\qquad\text{Gain}_{\text{abs}}=\mu_I-\mu_W$$

A positive value means improved stock grew faster (White, Adams & Neale 2007; Zobel & Talbert 1984).

9.2 The growth cohort — which trees count

All growth means (and therefore gain) use the surviving original cohort only: trees coded DEAD or DEAD (REPLACEMENT) are excluded. Interplanted replacements are a younger cohort, so including them would confound genetic gain with planting age. Mortality is analysed separately and does count those trees.

9.3 Unit of replication & significance

The independent unit is the seedlot (genetic-entry) mean, not the individual tree (plots are pseudo-replicates). A Welch two-sample t-test on seedlot means gives the p-value:

$$t=\frac{\mu_I-\mu_W}{\sqrt{\dfrac{s_I^2}{n_I}+\dfrac{s_W^2}{n_W}}},\qquad \nu=\frac{\left(\dfrac{s_I^2}{n_I}+\dfrac{s_W^2}{n_W}\right)^2}{\dfrac{(s_I^2/n_I)^2}{n_I-1}+\dfrac{(s_W^2/n_W)^2}{n_W-1}}$$

with Satterthwaite degrees of freedom \(\nu\). Stars: * p<0.05, ** p<0.01, *** p<0.001; ns = not significant. A 95% confidence interval on the difference is

$$(\mu_I-\mu_W)\;\pm\;t_{0.975,\nu}\sqrt{\tfrac{s_I^2}{n_I}+\tfrac{s_W^2}{n_W}}$$

9.4 Effect size — Hedges g

$$g=\frac{\mu_I-\mu_W}{s_p}\,J,\quad s_p=\sqrt{\frac{(n_I-1)s_I^2+(n_W-1)s_W^2}{n_I+n_W-2}},\quad J=1-\frac{3}{4(n_I+n_W)-9}$$

\(J\) is the small-sample bias correction. Roughly: |g| ≈ 0.2 small, 0.5 medium, 0.8 large.

9.5 Multiple comparisons — Benjamini–Hochberg FDR

With \(m\) sites tested, sort the p-values \(p_{(1)}\le\dots\le p_{(m)}\); the adjusted q-value is the monotone step-up:

$$q_{(i)}=\min_{k\ge i}\left\{\min\!\left(1,\;\frac{m}{k}\,p_{(k)}\right)\right\}$$

Enabling the FDR toggle controls the expected proportion of false "significant" calls across the visible family of sites.

9.6 Gain vs site productivity

An ordinary least-squares regression of absolute gain on the Woods Run site mean (a productivity proxy):

$$\text{Gain}_{\text{abs}}=\beta_0+\beta_1\,\mu_W+\varepsilon$$
Why absolute, not %. Gain % has \(\mu_W\) in its denominator, so regressing it on \(\mu_W\) manufactures a spurious negative slope. Absolute gain removes that coupling, so a negative slope here is a real signal (gains larger on poorer sites — a classic G×E pattern).

9.7 Mortality & G×E

$$\text{Mortality}\,\%=100\times\frac{\#\{\text{DEAD}\}+\#\{\text{DEAD (REPLACEMENT)}\}}{\text{trees}}$$

Cross-site ranking concordance of seedlots (genotype × environment) is summarised in reports with Kendall's W (0 = no agreement, 1 = identical rankings everywhere).

Screening tool. These are robust screening statistics. For inference-grade conclusions, follow up with a mixed-effects model (random site & seedlot effects).

11How-to recipes

A · Should we deploy Improved at one site?

  1. Open Plot Explorer; pick the Region and Installation.
  2. Read the deployment card verdict and its per-metric evidence.
  3. Scan the years (Year1→3) to check the gain holds up; glance at mortality in the KPIs.
  4. For the written case, ask ForestTask: "report on installation <NAME>".

B · Which sites show significant gain in volume, Year 2?

  1. Go to Genetic Gain & Summary; set Year = Year2, Metric = Volume, Site Type = CORE.
  2. In the dumbbell, look for gold rings; confirm in the site table's stars / p column.
  3. Worried about many tests? tick FDR-adjust and see which survive.
  4. Click Download gain table (CSV) to keep the numbers.

C · Compare the two regions

  1. Ask ForestTask: "report comparing INW vs K-S" (optionally name a metric).
  2. The report includes region trend charts and a side-by-side gain summary.

D · Track a single seedlot

  1. Ask ForestTask: "report on seedlot 97-72 across INW sites".
  2. You'll get its mean vs the source-group average, by site and year, plus stability.

12Setup & construction (for hosts / developers)

Architecture

A modular Dash app. Heavy lifting (86k rows → plot/seedlot/installation summaries + mortality) happens once at startup in a cached DataStore; callbacks then slice cached frames.

ModuleRole
data.pyLoads & validates the CSV once; pre-computes plot / seedlot / installation / mortality frames; growth frames exclude dead/replacement.
stats.pyRealized gain, Welch test + CI, Hedges g, Benjamini–Hochberg, productivity regression, deployment verdict, trial overview.
figures.pyThemed Plotly chart factories (heatmaps, dumbbell, scatter, boxplots, mortality, damage).
components.pyStyled tables, KPI cards, dropdowns.
layout.pyPage shell, filter bar, tabs, map pane, methods modal, ForestTask widget.
callbacks.pyAll interactivity (filters, tabs, chat, reports, downloads, URL state).
report.pyThe ForestTask report engine (parse → evidence → draft → critique → revise).
assistant.pyMindRouter (LLM) client and model routing.
map_builder.pyBuilds the Leaflet installations map at startup.

Data file schema

The app loads data/rgt24_new.csv (override with RGT_DATA_FILE). Required columns:

Region, Installation, Source, Seedlot, PLOT, TREE,
Replication, Year, Value, Metric, Management, Defect
  • Source ∈ {Woods run, Improved}; PLOT 1–3 = Woods, 4–6 = Improved.
  • Management carries DEAD / DEAD (REPLACEMENT) (drives mortality); Defect carries damage codes.
  • Metric ∈ {CALIPER GROWTH (MM), HEIGHT GROWTH (CM), VOLUME GROWTH (CM3)}.

Configuration & ops

  • MINDROUTER_API_KEY in a git-ignored .env enables ForestTask. The app runs without it (assistant shows "offline").
  • RGT_URL_PREFIX=/dashapp/ when serving under a subpath (matches nginx).
  • Tests & CI: python -m pytest -q runs golden-value tests for the gain math; a GitHub Actions workflow byte-compiles and tests on every push.

13Caveats & good practice

  • It's a screening tool. Confirm headline calls with a mixed-effects model before high-stakes decisions.
  • Year 3 is under-sampled — few sites measured; treat its averages as provisional.
  • Non-significant ≠ no effect — it means the data can't yet distinguish the gain from zero.
  • Multiplicity — with many sites, use the FDR toggle before trusting a handful of lone stars.
  • Improved often dies a little more — always weigh the survival panel against the growth gain.
  • Productivity proxy — "site quality" is the Woods Run mean, a within-trial proxy, not an external climate index.
